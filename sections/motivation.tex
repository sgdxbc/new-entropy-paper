\subsection{System Model and Goal}
\label{sec:motivation:model}

We assume multiple permissionless nodes contributing storage resources in DSNs, and the nodes follow a hybrid security model.
Among these nodes, we assume up to a certain fraction, for example \nicefrac{1}{3}, are Byzantine and the remaining nodes are rational~\cite{bar}.
Byzantine nodes may arbitrarily deviate from the expected behavior even if that causes them economic loss.
They may be malicious, attack the system, and attempt to cause data loss.
At the same time, rational nodes try to maximize their profits.
We assume an economic incentive mechanism that DSNs can employ to induce rational nodes to perform specific actions.
For example, in \sys nodes receive rewards for transmitting useful bits to clients, and we assume the reward exceeds the cost of persisting and transferring those bits, so rational nodes are incentivized to transmit correctly.
We will detail the prototype incentive design in \XXX.

Rational nodes follow the protocol only when doing so is most profitable.
For example, they may discard data copies they are responsible to store if they believe they will not be caught and penalized.
Therefore, DSNs must be able to verify the behaviors they want to incentivize, such as employing proofs of retrievability~\cite{por} to verify correct storage.
At the same time, rational nodes experience benign failures at some rate.
They unintentionally lose stored data for various reasons, including hardware wear out, catastrophic events, and churn.
These failures can be significant in heterogeneous storage, especially for resource constrained devices.
We define the faulty rate $f$ as the maximum fraction of Byzantine nodes and the failure rate $f_r$ as the average failed fraction of rational nodes during a time interval $T$.

Under this model, DSNs must keep data available while tolerating both $fN$ faulty nodes and $f_r(1-f)N$ failures from rational nodes during $T$, where $N$ is the total number of nodes.
Tolerating faulty nodes is more challenging than tolerating benign failures, because faulty nodes may strategically attack the system.
For example, they may try to choose the data they store, collude, and create correlated failures that lead to data loss.
We assume Sybil attacks can be mitigated, that is assuming a known $f$, by requiring nodes to stake to participate.
DSNs provide two APIs: \texttt{put} to store data with an expiration and obtain a reference, and \texttt{get} to retrieve data with the reference.
In all DSNs studied in this work, data hashes are used as references, so retrieved data can be validated with the reference.
Data availability means that between the return of \texttt{put} and data expiration, \texttt{get} can return the valid data successfully.

The goal of \sys is not only to ensure data availability but also to ensure it with a perfect redundancy level.
The redundancy level is the total storage units occupied by redundantly storing a data unit.
We require the redundancy level to be constant and not to grow with the number of nodes $N$, so the DSN can scale to massive numbers of nodes.
\sys also supports temporally varying $N$.
For simplicity, we treat $N$ in each time interval $T$ as constant and equal to the minimum number of nodes during $T$.
We assume $N$ for every time interval is known.

\subsection{Previous DSNs}
\label{sec:motivation:review}

In \autoref{tab:dsn} we summarize previous DSNs.
In this section, we examine their redundancy and data placement mechanisms and analyze their tolerance against Byzantine faults.

\paragraph{Storage markets.}
In Filecoin and Sia, clients sign storage contracts with nodes that rent their storage space for a fixed period.
The contracts are handled on blockchains, which are responsible for paying nodes after the rental period.
If nodes lose the data, the contracts are terminated immediately, and nodes lose their rewards and may be penalized.

In these DSNs, placement groups are materialized as storage contracts.
If clients sign contracts with faulty nodes, those nodes can drop the data and cause data loss.
The DSNs encourage clients to encode their data with erasure coding and sign contracts with multiple nodes, so single faults do not cause data loss.
However, the placement group size is not scalable, because DSNs must store each contract in costly blockchain state.
In Filecoin, data is typically redundantly stored on fewer than 10 nodes, and clients often pick nodes by comparing the storage prices they offer.
It is easy for faulty nodes to offer low storage prices to win contracts, so data can be exclusively stored by a set of faulty nodes.
In Sia, clients are configured to pick 30 nodes and store data with a \emph{10 out of 30} code by default, tolerating 20 faulty nodes.
This only mitigates the attack, and placement groups will eventually be compromised as the network scales to more nodes.
Worse, Sia relies on clients to detect node failures and repair redundancy loss, so clients must remain online at all times or their data may become unrecoverable.

\paragraph{Committee-based approach.}
In Walrus, 1000 nodes are selected as the committee to store all data during each two week epoch.
It applies a sophisticated erasure coding scheme called Red Stuff to minimize the repair overhead.
The encoded fragments are deterministically distributed to committee members, forming static placement groups for stored data.
With erasure coding, Walrus can tolerate up to \nicefrac{2}{3} faulty nodes in the committee.
However, Walrus cannot guarantee that there will not be more faulty committee members.
Walrus selects committee members by ranking their staked assets.
Faulty nodes will be selected as long as their staked assets exceed those of all other committee candidates except the top few hundred.
They can compromise the committee with a certain level of staking, which depends on the staking distribution of all committee candidates and is not necessarily a majority.
Besides, the total capacity of Walrus is limited by the capacities of top nodes, and the storage resources of unselected nodes cannot be utilized.

\paragraph{Integrate proofs of retrievability into consensus protocols.}
In these DSNs, nodes propose blocks to a blockchain to gain rewards.
The blocks must attach a proof of retrievability for certain data in order to be accepted by nodes and appended to the blockchain.
In Permacoin and Retricoin, blocks can prove retrievability to arbitrary segments of a large dataset that nodes agree to collectively archive.
In Arweave, a random historical block is sampled for each round, so the blockchain history itself is permanently archived.
These DSNs do not specify placement for the stored data, instead allowing nodes to freely choose the data they want to store.
This design avoids compromised placement groups in storage markets and committee based approaches, because faulty nodes cannot be exclusively responsible for storing some data.

The shortcoming of this incentive driven placement is high redundancy for availability.
Firstly, because these DSNs give up control on data placement, they can only store redundancy with replications so that nodes can increase data redundancy by simply synchronizing with each other.
Secondly, redundancy in these DSNs is automatically balanced across data.
If any data has more copies than others, the incentive to store it decreases due to competition, and storing nodes replace it with data that is more rarely stored.
Faulty nodes can strategically attack availability by storing copies of targeted data regardless of rewards.
If the average redundancy level, which is bounded by total storage capacity, is less than the number of faulty nodes, all rational nodes will cease to store the data.
The data will then be vulnerable to loss.
To avoid this situation, these DSNs must ensure that the redundancy level exceeds the number of faulty nodes and grows proportionally to the total number of nodes.
This severely restricts scalability.
